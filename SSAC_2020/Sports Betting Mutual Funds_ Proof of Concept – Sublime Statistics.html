Sports Betting Mutual Funds: Proof of Concept

March 29, 2020 MikeSoukup4 Leave a comment

The 2020 Sloan Sports Analytics Conference has come and gone and with that my opportunity to present Sports Betting Mutual Funds as part of the DraftKings Gaming Innovation Challenge. Although I presented my idea well, I was not victorious. The winning concept was prize-linked savings accounts presented by Liz Bloom.

For me, the nail in the coffin came during the Q&A. Jeff Ma was at the mic and we had some banter. I was fielding the questions well, but I knew the answer Jeff was looking for. After about a minute or two of back and forth he got straight to it, “Will users of this product win longterm?” My response: No.

This strikes a chord with me for a couple of reasons. First, I was presenting a new product offering on behalf of DraftKings. The whole point is to provide a product offering where the company can make money, not bleed them. Second, it is very rare for professional betters to make money long term. The game is rigged against them. I am not saying it cannot be done, but sports books are set up with a profitability somewhere between 5-8% depending on the sport. This means that those placing the bets are losing about 5-8% long term. It is a zero sum game after all. Third, I wanted to market this product to new betters, not those who think they have an edge. The key market is someone who is an opportunity better or doesn’t want to get taken for a ride while they are still learning so they will not loose it all on a single bet. It is a gateway betting product. Fourth, Sports Betting Mutual Funds are not intended to be held longterm. The max length would be a season, or about 4-5 months.

Because I was told I would only have 5 minutes to present, I left out a lot of the data evaluating these products. So, I wanted to take the time and defend myself a bit further by breaking out the analysis to show that these products do have some merit, and are innovative in their own right.

40 Years of Data

First, let’s look at a very macro-view of these products. In the image below, I tracked the performance of two funds that placed either all over or all under bets for every game in the NFL from 1979 until 2018.

There are a lot of takeaways here. This graph displays the ROI from a consumers perspective. Here you can clearly see the consumer’s long term loss and the books’ longterm profitability hovering around 4-5%. The Under fund had a life time return of -3.17% and the Over fund had a lifetime return of -5.75%. With that said, there were seasons where the user would turn profit. The biggest return for a consumer would have occurred in 1986 if they bought into the Under fund they would have seen a 9.13% return. On the flip side, the biggest lose would have been in the same year from buying into the Over fund losing 17.9% of the consumer’s capital.

Over the 40 seasons tracked here, the Over fund had net positive or neutral returns for 7 seasons, or 17.5% of the time. The Under fund had net positive or neutral returns for 13 seasons or 32.5% of the time.

This clearly does not show a winning strategy. Longterm you will lose money. Short term, you have the chance to win money or break even about 25% of the time. So this strategy will clearly not turn a doofus into a high roller, but it also won’t turn the aforementioned doofus into a chump by losing big either. A fee of 5% to get action on every game over the past 40 years is not bad, especially if it increases the viewers experience.

More Volatility, More Risk, More Reward

There are obvious issues with the betting strategies above. Knowing that sports books will return 5-8% of their holdings, it doesn’t make sense to cast as wide of a net as possible like you would do in the stock market. The underlying principals are different. The stock market generally increases by 6% a year while sports book generally profit 6% a year. If you buy into every game, of course you will lose about 6% because that is how the system is set up.

Instead, to make some money with a fund strategy in sports betting the scope has to be narrowed. To do this, I looked at betting on individual NFL teams against the spread for every game from 1979-2018. The narrowed focus of this approach does have some upside. Primarily, the rewards can be greater. You are only betting on 16 of the 256 games in an NFL season so you can profit from wins because you aren’t weighed down as much from the system. With the pros come the cons. The downside is that you can also stand to lose more money with this approach. Also, since you are not taking the field, there is a need for some additional intelligence and intuition to give an edge over the book.

As a case study for this approach, below I look at the fund performance for taking the Chicago Bears ATS for every game from 1979-2018.

Like the larger fund nets above, this fund too had a historic loss of around 5%. However, this fund turned a profit at a higher rate. Nearly 40% of the time, or 16 out of the last 40 seasons, buying into this fund would have turned a profit. Also, with some intelligence or faith, buying into this fund for one of the Bears’ breakout years like in 1985 or 2018 would have returned the holder 51.9% and 34.8% respectively. The loses were on the same scale and could be as bad as nearly 50%. But still that is not 100% and gives the holder some funds to fight another day.

Analyzing every NFL team over the last 40 years shows many variations, but also much of the same as above. There are lifetime loses as low as -14% (the Rams) but also two teams have generated lifetime positive returns. The first obvious one is the New England Patriots returning 1.18% to potential fund holders. The second sleeper is the Philadelphia Eagles squeaking out a net positive return of 0.06%.

I would argue that these team level funds could be very interesting for consumers. The rewards can be significant, the loses won’t break the bank, and it will give the consumer more of a reason to cheer for their favorite teams.

So, Would I Buy-In to One?

Short Answer: Yes.

Long Answer: It would have to be the right scenario. By no means am I a professional better. I do not have the insight to have a leg up on any book. So diversifying does make sense to me in this case. For me, a product like this would provide the excitement of betting without as much of the roller coaster highs and lows especially since it would be more of an entertainment option as opposed to a serious investment or lifestyle.

Also, being a lifetime sports fan, there are a couple of funds that I think would be very attractive. The first would be taking all underdogs on the moneyline for every game in the NCAA March Madness Round of 64. I love rooting for the underdogs in this tournament and I think the moneylines could really pay out and add to the excitement. Another would be a fund set up to take unders for all bowl games of the NCAA Bowl Season. Both of these scenarios have way too many teams for me to track any meaningful intelligence on all of the teams and players involved. But both would provide a way to increase my viewing pleasure during times of the year my eyes are normally glued to the TV.
